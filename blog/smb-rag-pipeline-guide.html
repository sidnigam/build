The SMB Guide to Building Your First RAG Pipeline

By Sid Nigam · February 3, 2026 · 9 min read

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) is the most practical way for SMBs to add AI to their workflows without training a model from scratch. You connect your existing documents — PDFs, wikis, support tickets — to a large language model, and it answers questions using your actual data. Here's how to do it in a weekend for under $200/month.

What is RAG and Why Does It Matter for SMBs?

A standard LLM like GPT-4 knows the world up to its training cutoff but nothing about your company. RAG fixes this by retrieving relevant chunks of your internal documents at query time and injecting them into the prompt. The result: an AI assistant that actually knows your products, policies, and processes.

The Stack (Under $200/Month)

  • LLM: OpenAI GPT-4o-mini (~$0.15/1M input tokens)
  • Embeddings: OpenAI text-embedding-3-small (~$0.02/1M tokens)
  • Vector DB: pgvector on a $20/month Supabase instance (or Pinecone free tier)
  • Orchestration: LangChain (open source)
  • Hosting: AWS Lambda + API Gateway (~$5/month for moderate use)

Step 1 — Ingest Your Documents

Use LangChain's document loaders to pull in your content (PDFs, Notion, Confluence, Google Docs). Chunk each document into ~500 token segments with 50-token overlap. Generate embeddings for each chunk and store them in pgvector with metadata (source, date, section).

Step 2 — Build the Retrieval Layer

At query time, embed the user's question, run a cosine similarity search against your vector store, and retrieve the top 5 most relevant chunks. Filter by metadata if needed (e.g., only search product docs, not HR policies).

Step 3 — Construct the Prompt

Inject the retrieved chunks into a system prompt along with instructions about tone, scope, and when to say "I don't know." This grounding step is what prevents hallucination — the model is constrained to answer from your documents.

Step 4 — Add a Feedback Loop

Log every query and response. Thumbs up/down from users feeds back into your evaluation pipeline. After 2 weeks, you'll have enough signal to identify gaps in your document coverage and prompt quality.
